Summary

Executive Summary

This project analyzes how visibility conditions affect departure delays at the three major New York airports (JFK, LGA, EWR), focusing on Boeing 737 and 757 aircraft during March 2013.


The key finding is that as visibility deteriorates, the entire distribution of departure delays shifts upward â€” indicating a systemic capacity constraint rather than isolated disruption events.


In the controlled OLS model, a one-mile improvement in visibility is associated with approximately 3.1 minutes less departure delay on average. In an operational scenario (10 miles vs. 2 miles visibility), expected delay increases by roughly 25 minutes.

Business Goal

In aviation operations, weather acts as a system-wide driver that reduces airport throughput. The objective was to produce a decision-ready estimate of the operational impact of poor visibility.

  • Quantify how visibility affects departure delays in minutes
  • Measure how visibility influences the probability of significant delays (>15 min)
  • Translate statistical coefficients into operational scenario impacts
  • Verify robustness against outliers and skewed delay distributions

The analytical pipeline follows a driver-to-impact logic: exploratory evidence â†’ controlled modeling â†’ scenario translation â†’ robustness validation.

Dataset & Preparation

The analytical dataset was constructed by merging flight records, aircraft data, and hourly weather observations:

  • Flights: departure times, delays, origin airport, hour
  • Planes: manufacturer and model â†’ identification of Boeing 737/757
  • Weather: visibility, wind speed, precipitation, temperature, pressure, humidity

Filtering and feature engineering

  • Restricted to March 2013
  • Selected Boeing aircraft with 737 or 757 model codes
  • Removed rows missing departure delay or visibility
  • Created visibility categories for exploratory analysis
  • Created binary variable for delays exceeding 15 minutes

Exploratory Analysis (EDA)

Boxplots of departure delays by visibility category show that worsening visibility raises not only extreme delays but the entire distribution.

  • Median delays increase as visibility declines
  • Distribution shift indicates reduced operational capacity

This confirms that the phenomenon exists in raw data before modeling.

Modeling Approach

Two complementary models were estimated:

  • OLS regression (HC3 robust errors): effect on average delay in minutes
  • Logistic regression: effect on probability of significant delay (>15 min)

Both models controlled for airport, hour of day, and additional weather variables to isolate the independent effect of visibility.
